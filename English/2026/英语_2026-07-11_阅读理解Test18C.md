## 【完整原文】

**C**

For four lonely years, Evelyn Jones of Rockford, Illinois, lived friendless and forgotten in one room of a cheap hotel. "I wasn't sick, but I was acting sick," the 78-year-old widow says, "Every day was the same. I would just lie on my bed and maybe cook up some soup." Then, six months ago, she was invited to The Brighter Side—Rockford's day care center for the elderly. Every weekday morning since then, she has left her home to meet nine other old people in a church for a rich program of charity work, trips, games, and—most important of all—friendly companionship.

　　Just a few years ago, there were few choices for the elderly between a normal life in their own homes and being totally confined in nursing homes. Many of them were sent to rest homes long before they needed full-time care. Others like Mrs. Jones were left to take care of themselves. But in 1971, the White House Conference on Aging called for the development of alternatives to care in nursing homes for old people, and since then, government-supported day-care programs like The Brighter Side have been developed in most big American cities.

　　"This represents a real alternative to the feared institution and makes old people believe they have not left the world of living," says Alice Brophy, 64, director of New York City's Office for the Aging, "They do well at the centers, and I hate it when people describe us as elderly playpens (婴儿护栏)." New York's 138 centers encourage continuing contact for the aged with the community's life. The centers serve more than 15,000 members, and volunteer workers are always looking for new ones. If someone doesn't show up at the center for several days in a row, a worker at the center calls to make sure all is well. And although participation in the center is free, those who want to can pay for their lunches.

　　No normal studies have been made of these centers for the elderly, but government officials are enthusiastic. In the future, the Public Health Service will do a study to decide if the programs can receive federal Medicare money. And the old people themselves are very happy with the programs. "There is no way," says Evelyn Jones, smiling at her new companions at the Brighter Side, "that I will ever go back to spending my day with all those lost at the hotel."

## 【逐题错误分析】

### 第1题 ❌ A urgent need of full-time care → ✅ C they were sent there
- 原句：Many of them **were sent** to rest homes **long before they needed full-time care**.
- 分析：关键在 **long before they needed full-time care**（早在他们**还不需要**全天候护理之前，就被送去了）。这恰恰说明他们**并非"急需(urgent need)"全护**，而是**被动"被送去(were sent)"**。C 直接对应原文 were sent。
- 你的失误：A 的"urgent need of full-time care"与原文"long before they needed"**完全相反**！这是阅读题最典型的**反义干扰项**——看到 full-time care 就选，没注意前面的 long before they needed（还没到需要的时候）。
- **技巧：细节题要读完整句，尤其 before/until/not 等限定词会彻底改变句意。**

### 第2题 ❌ C old people like nursing institutions → ✅ B do well at the day care centers
- 原句：Alice Brophy 说 "They **do well** at the centers, and I hate it when people describe us as elderly playpens."
- 分析：Alice 明说老人在**日托中心(centers)过得好(do well)**，B 直接复现。
- 排除其他项：
  - A：她**讨厌(hate)**别人把中心说成"婴儿护栏(playpens)"，所以不是 A
  - C：她说的是老人喜欢**day care centers**，不是 **nursing institutions**（养老院，正是文中"feared institution 令人害怕的机构"）——**偷换概念**！
  - D：无依据
- 你的失误：C 把"日托中心"偷换成"养老院(nursing institutions)"。原文 day care center（日托，正面）和 nursing home/institution（养老院，负面/feared）是**对立概念**，不能混。

## 📌 阅读理解技巧小结（针对你的错误）

**① 细节题：读完整句，警惕"限定词反转"**（第1题）
- were sent **long before they needed** full-time care = 还没到需要就被送去
- 干扰项 A 直接说"urgent need"，与原文相反 → 反义陷阱
- **before / until / not / hardly 等词会颠倒句意，绝不能只看半句**

**② 推断题：回原话定位，防"概念偷换"**（第2题）
- Alice 原话 "They do well at the centers" → B
- 干扰项 C 把 **day care center（日托,正面）** 偷换成 **nursing institution（养老院,负面/feared）**
- **本文核心对立：day care center 好 ↔ nursing home 令人害怕(feared institution)。看清主语是哪个！**

**③ 态度题：找情感词**（第3题你对了）
- officials **enthusiastic**、old people **very happy**、Evelyn 笑着说再也不回酒店 → Optimistic 乐观

**④ 主旨题：抓全文中心**（第4题你对了）
- 全文用 Evelyn 例子证明日托中心让老人生活更好 → B
